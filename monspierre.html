The Tablet of Monspierre

First Revision

22 May 2017

Roger stepped through the doorway into the cavern. A stone-hewn tablet, inscribed in some long-forgotten eldritch tongue, stood proudly atop the tall pyramid that dominated the room. In the shadow of the tablet, sitting just before it's pedestal, was an ebony coffin. The Tomb of Monspierre.

He took another awestruck step towards the pyramid and paused. Where before the air had been still, there now was a slight breeze. Not from the entrance to the cave behind, or from any crenelation in the rock above, but from the direction of the tablet itself. He took a more cautious step. The breeze became a wind. Roger marched forwards. With every step, the wind became fiercer and fiercer. He shut his eyes against the burning air.

"The Tablet of Monspierre?" Roger said, "That sounds like something from a Poe story."

"Who?" the barber asked, snipping off another lock of Roger's hair.

"You know, the guy who wrote The Raven and The Telltale Heart. Those old horror stories about people going insane."

The barber had no reply except to keep snipping.

"Once upon a midnight dreary ... never mind. So what does this tablet do?"

"It has the power to cure any illness."

"Right. So where is it, then?" Roger asked.

"Well, when Monspierre died, he had his most faithful servant bury him alongside the tablet in a secret tomb somewhere in Southern France. Nobody has ever managed to locate it" the barber replied.

"I see," Roger said. Vin had been his barber for years, and he always had a new story to tell when Roger came to visit.

"And on that note, you're done," Vin said.

Roger pulled off his barber's cloth and shook Vin's wrinkled hand. "Thanks for the story," He rustled through his pockets and produced a similarly wrinkled twenty. "And for the haircut."

Roger squinted. He took a raggedy step, his foot landing on the very bottom of the pyramid. He had to lean into the wind to avoid being blown back. Left foot passed right foot and he moved onto the pyramid's second step.

"Sick? How sick?" he said. He didn't notice it, but his foot was tapping at record pace.

"Leukemia," his mother replied.

"And I'm only hearing about this now?!" he cried, "you're dying!"

"I didn't want to worry you. I know you get nervous sometimes, and -"

"Nervous! You thought I would be less nervous when I'm ambushed with this information? You're DYING!"

Roger's mother bent her head and let tears fall to her lap. Roger had never seen her cry before.

He felt a sensation in his toes, almost like the feeling of cracking your knuckles, but far more painful. The bones were breaking. Another step.

"Are you OK, Roger?" someone said over his shoulder. Roger jumped, and reached for the mouse so he could close his browser window. But he had been staring at an empty desktop. He turned in his chair to face the shoulder-looker - his boss, Linderman.

"Fine."

Linderman knelt beside his chair, like a grown-up talking to a child. "Listen, I heard about your mother. Take some time off," he said, his brow scrunched up in the mask of false worry.

Roger took hold of his chair-arm in a thunderous grip. Hitting his boss was an all-around bad idea, but the urge to do it was nearly uncontrollable.

Linderman seemed not to notice. "I care about my employees, Roger. And you've been with us for years. If you don't want to do it for yourself, think of it as a favour to me."

A favour? Roger's mother was dying and Linderman was asking for a favour. How rich.

Linderman finally took notice of Roger's demeanor. "Are you sure that you're OK? You look very ill."

A frail voice spoke in his head. Why are you so angry? He's just trying to help. It was soon drowned out by unreasonable anger.

Roger's ankles snapped.

Beyond the steel mesh, the front door of the car opened. A blue-capped head peeked in.

"Your boss wanted to let you know that he won't press charges," the police officer said, "but you're on forced leave for two weeks."

Roger said nothing. After an expectant moment, the head ducked out and Roger fell to his side in the backseat, staring mindlessly at the dirt and dust littering the floor. His wrists were handcuffed, and his phone had been taken away. After a pause, the solemn silence was broken by a voice that somehow whispered at him from the nape of his neck.

The Tablet of Monspierre, it said.

He was on his knees, crawling up the jagged steps, tablet now only a few meters away. His clothing was long gone, currently plastered against the far wall of the tomb. His well-tied shoes were all that had managed to hold on to his body. They flapped on the broken ends of his ankles like a flag on a tall building, unhindered by any joint or bone.

Rock after rock came tumbling down past him. His fingers were almost broken, and yet he had barely made a dent. Before Roger stood an enormous pile of rubble, beyond which supposedly lied the Tomb of Monspierre.

There was a rumbling behind him. His faithful guide, Eduardo, pulled up beside the rubble in their Jeep.

"I have zem, brother," he said, stepping out. He threw open the backseat and pulled out an armful of sturdy wooden planks.

Roger took them silently and climbed onto the pile. He was no engineer, but his intuition guided him to the right decisions. The first plank here, it said. Roger complied, vertically driving the plank into the rubble below. The end protruded slightly, containing a small drilled hole. And the second here, intuition said again from the nape of his neck. Roger complied. By the end, five planks had been placed throughout the pile.

Eduardo tied a rope to the hitch on the back of the Jeep. With Roger's help, they wound it tightly through each plank-hole.

"Vish me luck, brother," Eduardo said, climbing into the front seat. He revved the engine, and slammed the car forwards. The planks tore through the rubble, casting it aside in a rocky explosion.

Hardly waiting for the debris to settle, Roger sprinted from his place of cover and back to the now much reduced pile of stones. He drove his bare hands in, and the rocks moved easier than ever. Eventually, a tight channel formed. He could taste the air rushing from the cave. It tasted like dust.

Roger's fingers were inches from the tablet when all of the bones in his hand broke at once, and the skin tore away from his face, leaving a red skull with two spherical, bloodshot eyeballs sitting inside.

As the ancient air flowed past his face, his phone rang. He ignored it, and focused on widening the channel. After it stopped ringing, it vibrated. A text message. He deviated from the widening for a moment to check it.

Dr. Chanel
Roger, your mother is in urgent care now. She doesn't have long. You need to come.

Roger repocketed and kept widening the channel with increased fervor.

Eduardo stood, antsy, by the Jeep. Not a sound had come from the cave since Roger entered half an hour past. Nervous, he decided to follow.

"Brother?" he called. No reply. "Roger?"

He moved down the rocky, moisture-worn steps. Running his hand along the wall as he went, black dust collected on his fingertips like graphite. He noticed that the cave was constantly lit in a soft light. Looking around, however, he could find no source for it.

Up ahead was the doorway.

He stepped through and saw nothing. An empty room. Definitely human-carved, but completely unfurbished. No coffin or casket anywhere in sight, not even a pile of loose dirt. So much for the Tomb of Monspierre. More importantly though - no Roger. He turned back to the doorway - perhaps he had missed a fork in the stairs, or another doorway.

The back wall came into view and Eduardo stopped.

​ Nestled in the corner where rough floor met rocky wall, just left of the entrance doorway, was a crumpled white shirt. Roger's shirt. And near it, his shorts. And, after a moment, Eduardo realized that this wall was not the same color as the others. It was splattered in bright red.
